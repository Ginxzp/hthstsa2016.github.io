INTRODUCTION TO ENGINEERING DESIGN

Each year, freshmen receive their first experience in the field of engineering through Ms. Linda Grunthaner's Introduction to Engineering Design course, taught using the Project Lead The Way curriculum. In this class, students are taught the principles of design and sketching as they develop their own personal portfolios. After gaining an understanding of the basic sketching concepts, the students move on to CADD computer modeling and physical prototyping using Autodesk Inventor. Students learn basic shop skills and tool safety to prepare them for future engineering classes and projects. The course also delves into the topics of production and marketing in engineering fields. Students who complete the course successfully are able to apply for college credit through the Rochester Institute of Technology.

PRINCIPLES OF ENGINEERING

Principles of Engineering, a course taught by Mr. Christopher Hanas, continues the exploration of basic engineering values with a focus on mechanical engineering. The course is heavily project-based; each unit begins with a short briefing or instruction on a topic by Mr. Hanas, followed by a problem that must be solved independently by the students. These problems involve the design of mechanical, electrical, and structural engineering systems, such as building a bridge, designing a car, and constructing a tower. Quizzes and unit tests are given periodically to help reinforce the analytic problem-skills used to model physical designs.

In the bridge-building project, students construct simulation bridges with the use of the West Point Bridge Design Software. Upon using the various aspects of this immense software program, students are able to experiment with a multitude of different construction materials, dimensions, and other physical properties to construct the most durable, yet cost-efficient bridge in the entire class. In order to test the performance of each student, the program runs a simulation to test whether or not the bridge is able to withstand a specific load.

The car construction project involves the construction of a mousetrap-powered vehicle designed for speed and efficiency. Students are instructed to design self-powered cars that must meet certain size, material, and mobility specifications. Similar to the bridge design, both cost effectiveness and quality are considered in the determination of the winner. The project integrates material from the sophomore physics curriculum, including a hands-on lab activity, to help tie in theoretical knowledge.

One of the most fascinating projects in the Principles of Engineering curriculum is the Soccer Trophy Project. This project is introduced to the students on the first day of class as the "ice-breaker" project for the course. The project requires students to create a structure that can hold up a one-pound soccer ball eight feet in the air (with a tolerance of +/- 2 inches) for at least 4 days. As of 2011, only five groups have completed this challenge in the 18-year history of High Technology High School. The project demands that students work together and understand the physical environment in which they are building their structure.

COMPUTER INTEGRATED MANUFACTURING

Computer Integrated Manufacturing is a course taught by Mr. Robert Dennis. It relates computer aided drafting and design (CADD), prototyping, and manufacturing processes. Students are able to use the latest version of Autodesk Inventor software to model and render everyday objects and machine parts. Later in the course, the students learn CNC programming to relay mathematical coordinates and commands to an advanced milling center that uses a high-speed bit to rapidly carve out physical objects. The milling machine receives numerical instructions for milling and creates an object entirely without human interaction. Students take this knowledge to another level by writing programs for the school's robot to carry out simple tasks and instructions as part of the Pick 'n' Place Project.

DIGITAL ELECTRONICS

The Digital Electronics class is taught by Mr. Peter Borchardt and deals with the applied logic that is a major part of the application of electronic circuits and devices. Computer simulation software is used to design and test digital circuitry before the students construct the actual circuits with the aid of breadboarding kits. As the final project for the year, students are challenged to find or create a problem which they will then use a circuit to solve. For this project, students must work in pairs and submit written documentation along with the circuit to the teacher for a final assessment.
